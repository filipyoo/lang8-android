
The Usage of ものを and のに

When ものを or のに are used with a conditional clause, they are used to stress your regression for the past actions. The conditional clause describes actions you didn't do, and the clause that includes ものを or のに describes expected results that is obtained if you made the right decision. ものを is more formal than のに.

昨日のパーティに行けば、彼に会えたものを。
昨日のパーティに行けば、彼に会えたのに。
If I went to the yesterday's party, I could see him. 

去年あの株を買っていれば、今頃大金持ちになっていたものを。
去年あの株を買っていれば、今頃大金持ちになっていたのに。
If I bought that stock last year, I'd be a rich now.

もっと一生懸命勉強していたら、試験に落ちなかったものを。
もっと一生懸命勉強していたら、試験に落ちなかったのに。
If I studied harder, I didn't fail the exam.

All of the above Japanese sentences are used to blame someone's past actions.


Even if you drop ものを or のに, the fact which is described in the each sentence isn't changed. However, the listener can't understand whether the speaker regret it or not.
昨日のパーティに行けば、彼に会えた。
去年あの株を買っていれば、今頃大金持ちになっていた。
もっと一生懸命勉強していたら、試験に落ちなかった。


You can also use them to bemoan someone's current actions, they come after the present tense of verbs in this case.

あの株を今買えば、将来大金持ちになれるものを。
あの株を今買えば、将来大金持ちになれるのに。
If you bought that stock now, you'd be a rich in the future. (Why don't you buy it? You should buy it now.)

明日のパーティに行けば、彼に会えるものを。
明日のパーティに行けば、彼に会えるのに。
If you go to the tomorrow's party, you can see him. (Why won't you go? You should go.)
