
The Usage of はずだ and ことになっている

はずだ and ことになっている are used to describe someone's plans. The following two sentences sound natural.

彼は２時に来ることになっている。
彼は２時に来るはずだ。
He is supposed to come at two o'clock.

In my feeling, "ことになっている" is used when there is a 100 % possibility; "はずだ" is used when there is an about 90% possibility. 
If you are going to be transferred to Japan next month, you can say "来月、日本に転勤することになっています." "転勤するはずです" sounds unnatural; if you say so, the listener would wonder that "Why you don't know your own schedule well?"  If you aren't told about the transfer but everybody think that you'll be transferred from various situations, you should say "転勤するはずです."


はずだ is also used to show your strong expectations.

あなたは、試験に受かるはずだ。
I know/believe you'll pass the exam.

本田が加入したので、ミランは優勝するはずだ。
Since Honda has joined, AC Millan will be the champion this season.

In these cases, "ことになっている" sounds weird, ”受かることになっている" and "優勝することになっている" sound as if some queer transactions have done. 


はずだ is also used to blame someone by describing a fact. If you call your client on Sunday, he might tell you "今日は日曜日のはずだ (Today is Sunday, isn't it?) What he wants to say is "You shouldn't call me on Sunday."
If you can't solve an easy math problem, your teacher might say "この問題は小学生にも解けるはずだぞ." That means "This problem is expected to be solved by elementary school children" and implies "Why you don't solve such an easy problem?"


You can use "はずの" and "ことになっている" to modify nouns.

会議に出るはずの上司がまだ来ない。
会議に出ることになっている上司がまだ来ない。
My boss, who is supposed to attend this meeting, hasn't come yet.

The past tense and the future tense of "はずの" and "ことになっている" can be used to describe past events.
会議に出る はずの 上司が来なかった。
会議に出る ことになっている 上司が来なかった。
会議に出る はずだった 上司が来なかった。
会議に出る ことになっていた 上司が来なかった。
My boss, who was supposed to attend that meeting, didn't come.
